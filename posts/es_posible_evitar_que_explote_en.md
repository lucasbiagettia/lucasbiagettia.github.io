# Is it possible to prevent the bubble from bursting?

A few days ago I published [some reflections](https://lucasbiagetti.com.ar/posts_html/hacia-un-marco-linguistico_en.html) on how the metaphors we pile onto AI produce a lot of confusion. This confusion could be defined as follows: the words we are using lead to mistaken intuitions about what AI can do, as if we were almost creating synthetic humans.

That confusion is having an enormous economic impact, costing a lot of money and potentially leading to a massive disappointment: what we call "bursting the bubble".

In this article I express my view on what is happening and how the market could readjust its direction to avoid losses.

## What Anthropic says about its own product

Recently Anthropic published a [report](https://www.anthropic.com/research/labor-market-impacts) highlighting the huge gap between the theoretical capabilities of AI models and their actual adoption to replace or assist with tasks done by humans.

There are compelling data points. For example, even in computing and mathematics—the most exposed category—Claude covers only 33% of tasks. In most occupational categories, real coverage is minimal. They did not find a systematic increase in unemployment in the most exposed occupations. The only faint signal is a slight slowdown in hiring 22 to 25 year olds, and the paper itself warns that it has multiple interpretations.

This is not said by a skeptic. It is said by the company building the models and that has every incentive in the world to inflate expectations.

## The problem is about direction, not time or processing capacity

There is an interpretation suggesting that artificial intelligence does not replace humans YET, and that what it still needs in order to do so is development time or processing power.

I propose a different view: what we call artificial intelligence is a group of technologies that are impressive for certain things, but not necessarily for replacing human intelligence.

The “synthetic human” narrative—fueled by the metaphors I described in my [previous text](https://lucasbiagetti.com.ar/posts_html/hacia-un-marco-linguistico_en.html): AI "learns", "reasons", "understands”—has led us to evaluate this technology with the wrong yardstick. The success metric is how many humans are replaced.

On one project, a client asked us to use AI to replace 70% of his call center. His idea of success was fewer people, lower costs. At the time I wasn't as clear on all this as I am here, but mostly out of fear of failure, I proposed a different metric: customer satisfaction. Changing the direction of the definition of success looks like what I'm proposing here.

----

I like to think of language models as a gigantic hyper-compressed map of the universe of human language, which makes them, at least to me, incredible pattern detectors and fillers. And that capability, extended beyond language, is where the value lies that the market is not seeing.

An LLM trained on language can detect communication patterns associated with fraud. But the same logic extrapolates: a model trained on financial transactions can analyze millions of operations in real-time and flag anomalies a human team would take weeks to find. A model fed with industrial sensor data can anticipate machinery failures before they occur. One trained on logistics data can reorganize distribution routes by simultaneously processing more variables than any person could handle. One that cross-references thousands of legal documents can flag inconsistencies in a fraction of the time it would take a legal team.

None of those use cases, which I thought of in five minutes by iterating with (precisely) an LLM, appear in the headlines. None generate the fantasy of massive replacement. And yet, they are where AI is delivering real, measurable value today.

## The wrong focus leads to the burst

An [MIT study](https://nanda.media.mit.edu/ai_report_2025.pdf) (*The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025*) found that 95% of corporate generative AI implementations failed to generate a measurable ROI. Companies invested between 35 and 40 billion dollars in AI projects, and almost all of them saw zero impact on their bottom line. The 5% that did succeed focused on solving a specific problem, not on replacing people.

That stat should be read alongside the Anthropic report. AI isn't failing because it's bad technology. It's failing because it's being applied with the wrong expectations. And if investment continues to chase the replacement narrative, the resulting disappointment will drag down the use cases where AI actually generates value along with it.

I'm not a market expert who can predict if a correction can be avoided. But I do know enough about this technology to say that the gap between what is expected and what gets done won't be closed the way the market imagines. Not because AI can't, but because we are asking it to be something it isn't, while ignoring what it already does extraordinarily well.
